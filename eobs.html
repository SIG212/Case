01 — Overview

The challenge

On most NHS wards, patient observations were still being recorded on paper. Nurses would write down vitals, manually calculate a NEWS2 early warning score, and then re-enter the same data into the electronic patient record. It was slow, prone to transcription errors, and meant that critical information wasn't always where it needed to be at the right moment.

The product we set out to build would let nurses record observations at the bedside — on a phone or tablet — with the data flowing directly into the patient's record. No duplicate entry. Scores calculated automatically. Clinical risk visible in real time.

Simple in concept. Complex in practice. Ward environments are noisy, understaffed, and high-pressure. The staff using this tool range from 22-year-old agency nurses picking up a shift, to healthcare assistants who'd been doing the job on paper for 20 years. The design had to work for all of them.

"The handwriting news chart is just taking ages. With eObs, it says the scores straight away — it's very good for people who don't know how to read the vital signs."
— Healthcare Assistant, ward-based user

03 — Key Insights

What we learned

Staff are overwhelmed — but motivated

Ward visits revealed chaotic environments: morning huddles disrupted by understaffing and agitated patients. Despite this, staff were genuinely motivated when they could see the tool helping their patients. The emotional connection to patient outcomes was a consistent theme.

"Obviously, if I see the patient gets well and they're not coming back — that's the most rewarding."

— Healthcare Assistant, ward-based user

Pain points vs. perceived value

😤 Pain points

  • Interface too complex and click-heavy
  • Connectivity lost mid-session
  • Users randomly logged out
  • No clear flow within the app
  • Agency staff cannot access eObs
  • Android technical issues
  • Missing native supported forms
  • NEWS2 frequency auto-changes unexpectedly

✅ Perceived value

  • Saves significant time vs. paper
  • Eliminates double data entry
  • NEWS2 scores visible immediately
  • Transparency between departments
  • Better patient safety via real-time data
  • Reduces risk of manual errors

Ward-specific findings

  • 🔒
    Forensic wards raised concerns about backdating observations — a 30-minute window was agreed as the safe limit, with mandatory reason codes to prevent misuse.
  • 🧓
    Elderly wards were enthusiastic about NEWS2 but required closer digital support due to lower tech confidence among staff.
  • 🌙
    Night shifts have distinct observation requirements — a dedicated night-time template was consistently requested.
  • ⏱️
    Therapeutic observations in mental health settings are assigned per hour (not per shift) — a fundamental workflow difference requiring dedicated UX treatment.
  • 💡
    Staff are creative and proactive: suggestions included voice recognition for notes, biometric login, and a football-style chart to visualise completed vs. missed observations.

Technology proficiency

21% of survey respondents rated themselves as only "somewhat proficient" with EHR systems. Older cohorts showed significantly lower digital confidence. This directly shaped the design principle: the interface must work for the least digitally confident user, not the average.

Patient profile — History
Patient Profile

A single screen combining patient identity, clinical alerts, and observation history — no navigation needed.

  • Alerts, allergies and NEWS2 always visible at the top
  • To do / History toggle keeps the interface uncluttered
  • History view uses a timeline to show missed and completed obs
  • Missed observations highlighted in pink for immediate visibility
  • Gender field added following forensic ward feedback
Tasks list
Tasks List

A cross-patient view of all pending observations — requested consistently across all sites. Staff needed to see what's overdue and what's coming without going patient by patient.

  • Overdue tasks surface immediately with clear visual urgency
  • Completion state shown inline (e.g. 1 out of 4 left)
  • Sortable and filterable by type, status, or time
Observation summary
Observation Summary

Before saving, staff see a full summary of the NEWS2 reading with per-vital scores — addressing the research finding that staff wanted scores visible as they enter data.

  • Per-vital scores shown alongside each reading
  • Overall NEWS2 score and trend arrow prominent
  • Observation Chart preview available before confirming
  • Single "Save observation" CTA — no ambiguity
05 — Design Decisions

Principles that shaped the product

🎯

Minimum clicks

Every flow was reviewed against the "too clicky" feedback. The core journey — select patient, record obs, save — had to be frictionless.

👁️

Progressive disclosure

Complex features (therapeutic obs, backdating, customisation) are secondary. The main screen surfaces only what staff need in the moment.

🌍

Accessible by default

Designed for non-UK nationals, varying tech literacy, and users with disabilities — not as an afterthought, but as a core constraint.

🏥

Ward-aware

Mental health, forensic, acute, and elderly wards have distinct workflows. The product supports ward-level configuration rather than a single rigid flow.

Key decisions

Backdating with accountability

A 30-minute backdating window with mandatory reason codes — balancing the legitimate clinical need (busy ward rounds) with safeguarding against misuse flagged by forensic ward staff.

Tasks & timeline views

Requested consistently across all sites. Staff need to see what's done, overdue, and coming — not just a patient list with no temporal context.

Therapeutic obs per hour

Mental health wards assign therapeutic observations by the hour, not the shift. This required a dedicated flow separate from the standard NEWS2 model.

Night-time template

Overnight observation requirements differ from daytime ones. A configurable night-time template was scoped following consistent feedback from ward staff.

What I'd do differently

There were moments where technical constraints pushed back on design decisions — and in a couple of cases I accepted compromises too early. In hindsight, some of those "edge case" flows that got simplified were actually the ones most likely to cause problems in real ward conditions. I'd push harder on those earlier.

I'd also have liked to do more post-launch observation. Most of what we learned came from sessions before or during development. Watching staff actually use the shipped product on a ward — not a prototype in a meeting room — would have been valuable.

The thing that stuck with me: staff weren't resistant to change. They were frustrated that the tools they'd been given for years hadn't been designed with them in mind. When something genuinely works for them, they embrace it immediately.

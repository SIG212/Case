01 — Overview

The challenge

On most NHS wards, patient observations were still being recorded on paper. Nurses would write down vitals, manually calculate a NEWS2 early warning score, and then re-enter the same data into the electronic patient record. Slow, error-prone, and a genuine clinical risk.

The product we set out to build would let nurses record observations at the bedside — on a phone or tablet — with data flowing directly into the patient's record. No duplicate entry. Scores calculated automatically. Clinical risk visible in real time.

Simple in concept. Complex in practice. Ward environments are noisy, understaffed, and high-pressure. Staff range from 22-year-old agency nurses picking up a shift to healthcare assistants who'd been doing the job on paper for 20 years. The design had to work for all of them.

03 — Key Insights

What we learned

😤 What was getting in the way

  • Interface too complex, too many clicks
  • Connectivity lost mid-session
  • Users randomly logged out
  • No clear flow within the app
  • Agency staff cannot access eObs
  • Android technical issues
  • Missing native supported forms
  • NEWS2 frequency auto-changes unexpectedly

✅ What staff valued

  • Saves significant time vs. paper
  • Eliminates double data entry
  • NEWS2 scores visible immediately
  • Transparency between departments
  • Better patient safety via real-time data
  • Reduces risk of manual errors

Ward-specific findings

  • 🔒
    Forensic wards raised concerns about backdating — a 30-minute window was agreed as the safe limit, with mandatory reason codes to prevent misuse.
  • 🧓
    Elderly wards were enthusiastic about NEWS2 but required closer digital support due to lower tech confidence among staff.
  • 🌙
    Night shifts have distinct observation requirements — a dedicated night-time template was consistently requested.
  • ⏱️
    Therapeutic observations in mental health settings are assigned per hour, not per shift — a fundamental workflow difference requiring dedicated UX treatment.
  • 💡
    Staff are creative: suggestions included voice recognition, biometric login, and a football-style chart to visualise completed vs. missed observations.

05 — Design Decisions

Principles that shaped the product

🎯

Minimum clicks

Every flow was reviewed against the "too clicky" feedback. Select patient, record obs, save — frictionless.

👁️

Progressive disclosure

Complex features are secondary. The main screen surfaces only what staff need in the moment.

🌍

Accessible by default

Designed for non-UK nationals, varying tech literacy, and users with disabilities — as a core constraint, not an afterthought.

🏥

Ward-aware

Mental health, forensic, acute, and elderly wards have distinct workflows. The product supports configuration, not one-size-fits-all.

What I'd do differently

There were moments where technical constraints pushed back on design decisions — and in a couple of cases I accepted compromises too early. Some of those "edge case" flows that got simplified were the ones most likely to cause problems in real ward conditions.

I'd also have liked to do more post-launch observation. Most of what we learned came before or during development. Watching staff use the shipped product on a ward — not a prototype in a meeting room — would have been valuable.

Staff weren't resistant to change. They were frustrated that the tools they'd been given for years hadn't been designed with them in mind. When something genuinely works for them, they embrace it immediately.
